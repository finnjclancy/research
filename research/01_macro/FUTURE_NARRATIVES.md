# Future Narratives — Superforecasting the Stories That Will Re-Rate the Book

> Forward-looking scenario work, applying the discipline of *Superforecasting* (Tetlock & Gardner) to the question: **what stories might the world start telling itself over 2026–2032, and which of my names re-rate if it does?** These are longer-horizon "super-scenarios"; the nearer-term branches live in [`MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md`](MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md). Research date 2026-06-29.
>
> ⚠ The probabilities here are **my reasoned judgments, not facts** — explicitly subjective, horizon-tagged, and meant to be *updated*. The point is not to be right today; it's to be **calibrated and to know what would change my mind.**

---

## Part 0 — How to forecast a narrative like a superforecaster

The book's findings, operationalised for *narrative* forecasting (which is what an equity analyst actually does — pricing the probability and magnitude of a story taking hold):

1. **Be a fox, not a hedgehog.** No single grand theory ("it's all AI", "it's all the debt cycle"). Hold many models loosely; let the most-relevant one lead per question. *This document is deliberately a fox's spread of ~18 narratives, not one big call.*
2. **Dragonfly eye.** Aggregate many viewpoints (bull, bear, policymaker, adversary, the crowd). The truth is usually a synthesis.
3. **Fermi-ize.** Decompose each fuzzy narrative into a few tractable sub-questions you *can* track (e.g., "AI bust" → does a hyperscaler cut capex? does a neocloud default? does AWS growth roll over?).
4. **Outside view before inside view.** Start with the base rate ("how often do capex super-cycles end in a bust?", "how often do great-power chokepoint conflicts actually trigger?"), *then* adjust for the specifics. Most forecasting errors are neglected base rates.
5. **Granular probabilities, horizon-tagged.** Not "likely/unlikely" — a number, with a date. "30%-ish by 2030" beats "could happen." Round numbers are a tell of low resolution; I'll use ranges.
6. **Signposts > predictions.** The deliverable isn't the guess; it's the **falsifiable leading indicators** that tell you the narrative is arriving — *before* the crowd. Each narrative below lists the tells.
7. **Premortem / consider the opposite.** For every story, write the strongest case it's *wrong* and the kill criteria. Actively seek disconfirming evidence.
8. **Update incrementally (Bayesian).** Many small updates as signposts fire, not one big flip. Don't anchor; don't over- or under-react to single data points (signal vs noise).
9. **Separate the story from the wager.** A narrative can be *true* and already *priced* (no edge), or *unlikely* but *massively mispriced* (huge edge). The job is the gap between the world and the price.
10. **Scenarios correlate.** Real risk isn't one narrative — it's the **chains** (e.g., Taiwan shock → AI bust → fiscal-dominance response). See the synthesis in Part 2.

**Template per narrative:** *the story · outside view · P(×horizon) · signposts to watch · book read-through · the premortem (what kills it).*

## Part 1 — The narratives

# Cluster A — How the AI build-out resolves
*The single biggest shared exposure in the book (ASML, AMD, Amazon, ABB, Enel, Iberdrola). It will not resolve into one outcome — these are competing/overlapping end-states.*

### A1 — Sovereign AI: the state becomes the builder *(the seed example)*
- **The story:** AI compute is reclassified as **strategic national infrastructure** — like the interstate, the grid, or nuclear. Governments fund, mandate, or take equity in domestic compute; data-residency and "no foreign national" rules harden (the [Fable 5 ban](AI_STACK_WARS.md) was the first shot). Already concrete: **Project Stargate (~$500bn, OpenAI/Oracle/SoftBank/MGX)**, Oracle's $150bn+ "sovereign cloud" capex, **Stargate UAE/G42**, and national programs in Japan, Canada, France ([FinancialContent](https://markets.financialcontent.com/stocks/article/tokenring-2026-2-5-the-500-billion-blueprint-how-project-stargate-is-redefining-ai-as-national-infrastructure), [computeforecast](https://www.computeforecast.com/long-reads/sovereign-ai-infrastructure-nation-state-competition/)).
- **Outside view:** states *always* nationalise the commanding heights of a perceived security technology (rail, telecom, nuclear, space, 5G). Base rate of "strategic tech → state involvement" is high; the question is degree/speed, not direction.
- **P:** "Sovereign AI" is a *dominant* policy frame and a real demand stream **by 2028: ~80%**; outright government *ownership stakes* in major AI/compute firms becoming common **by 2030: ~45%**.
- **Signposts:** more national "AI infrastructure" funds; export-control tightening; data-residency mandates; government equity stakes (Intel precedent → OpenAI talks); defense-budget lines for compute; allied "compute blocs."
- **Book read-through:** **structurally bullish the picks-and-shovels** — ASML (litho is the ultimate chokepoint, agnostic to *who* builds), Amazon/AWS (sovereign-compliant cloud demand), ABB + Enel/Iberdrola (the power + electrical kit every sovereign data centre needs). Raises **policy-beta** on all of them (they trade on headlines, not just earnings). A floor under the capex story.
- **Premortem (what kills it):** an AI demand/ROI bust (A3/A4) makes governments *cut* rather than build; fiscal strain (C1) crowds it out; or compute commoditises so much that "sovereignty" is cheap and undramatic.

### A2 — The power bottleneck: electrons, not chips, become the binding constraint
- **The story:** the gating factor on AI shifts from GPUs to **electricity and grid**. Data-centre power goes from ~4% of US electricity today toward **~12% by 2030 (+1 trillion kWh/yr)**; PJM capacity costs are already doubling; **transformer lead times have gone from ~2 to ~5 years**; the "grid cliff" hits **2027–2028** ([Morgan Stanley](https://www.morganstanley.com/insights/articles/powering-ai-energy-market-outlook-2026), [Cornford & Cross](https://cornfordandcross.com/market-insights/the-power-bottleneck-ai-data-centers-and-the-grid-cliff-approaching-2027-2028/)). Utilities and electrical-equipment makers get re-rated as **AI-infrastructure plays**, and a **nuclear/SMR renaissance** kicks off (≥5GW dedicated nuclear for data centres by 2030; Three Mile Island restart).
- **Outside view:** bottlenecks migrate down the value chain in every build-out (rail→steel, internet→fiber, EVs→batteries/lithium). Whatever is *scarcest and slowest to build* captures economic rent. Power infra is slow (permits, transformers, transmission) → high pricing power.
- **P:** power as *the* recognised binding constraint **by 2027: ~70%** (it half-is already); a sustained utility/electrical-equipment **re-rating on the AI-power theme persisting through 2028: ~60%**.
- **Signposts:** PPA prices; transformer/turbine lead times; data-centre interconnection queues; nuclear restarts & SMR orders; utility capex guides; hyperscaler "behind-the-meter" generation deals.
- **Book read-through:** **the cleanest beneficiaries are Iberdrola & Enel** (already taking *equity* in data centres — see their [narrative](../05_narratives/IBERDROLA_narrative.md)) and **ABB** (triple-digit data-centre electrification orders). This is the narrative that can let the **rate-sensitive utilities re-rate *up* despite high rates** — the most underappreciated positive in the book.
- **Premortem:** efficiency gains (better chips/cooling, algorithmic efficiency, on-device inference) flatten the power curve; an AI bust collapses demand; or the re-rating is *already* fully priced (Iberdrola trades above target).

### A3 — The AI financing accident: the build-out ends in a *credit* event, not an earnings miss
- **The story:** the bust comes through the **financial plumbing**, not the P&L. Hyperscaler capex (~$600–725bn in 2026) is increasingly **debt-funded**; **neoclouds carry GPU-collateralised debt** (CoreWeave ~$14.2bn); **private credit to AI has gone from ~$0 → $200bn+, with MS projecting +$800bn more in two years**; ratings are anchored to single customer contracts; and **~95% of corporate AI projects show no measurable ROI** ([sourceryintel](https://sourceryintel.com/reports/ai-infrastructure-financial-bubble), [junkbondinvestor](https://www.junkbondinvestor.com/p/credit-weekly-the-anatomy-of-ai-infrastructure)). The **Fed lists AI as a top systemic risk.** A neocloud default or a circular-financing unwind (Nvidia's ~$40bn vendor equity — Burry's "Enron/Cisco" warning, see [AI_STACK_WARS §4](AI_STACK_WARS.md)) cascades into a sector-wide de-rating that *overshoots* fundamentals.
- **Outside view:** capex super-cycles funded by leverage on rapidly-depreciating collateral (GPUs depreciate faster than the loans) have a **high historical bust rate** (railroads, telecom/fiber 2000, shipping). The mechanism is textbook.
- **P:** a *named* AI-financing stress event (neocloud default / forced GPU fire-sale / circular-deal unwind) **by 2028: ~40–50%**; it escalating to a *broad* risk-off that re-rates the whole complex **by 2028: ~25–30%**.
- **Signposts:** neocloud refi terms / GPU-backed loan spreads; hyperscaler capex *guide cuts* (Amazon first — the cleanest trigger, see [Amazon narrative](../05_narratives/AMAZON_narrative.md)); HY/IG issuance share that is AI-linked; data-centre REIT/lease vacancy; a marquee "AI ROI disappointment" headline; depreciation-schedule write-downs.
- **Book read-through:** **the highest-impact downside** — ASML & AMD (their order books *are* this capex), Amazon (the spend epicentre), ABB + utilities (lose the demand leg). Because of the leverage, the move overshoots. This is **S3 in the [scenario tree](MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md)**, here given its credit-market transmission.
- **Premortem:** AI revenue actually shows up (AWS reaccel, real enterprise ROI); rates fall (C-cluster) and refinance the debt cheaply; hyperscalers fund from cash flow not debt → no collateral spiral.

### A4 — Token deflation / margin migration: "intelligence too cheap to meter"
- **The story:** open weights + MoE efficiency + competition collapse the price of intelligence (Chinese open models already ~60% of OpenRouter tokens, 5–30× cheaper — see [AI_STACK_WARS §2](AI_STACK_WARS.md)). The **model layer commoditises**; margin migrates to **hardware (Nvidia), the application layer, and power**. The *revenue* that justifies the capex never materialises at the model layer → feeds A3, *or* — the optimistic fork — **Jevons paradox**: cheap inference explodes total usage and *raises* aggregate compute demand.
- **Outside view:** technology prices fall ~ relentlessly (Moore's law, bandwidth, storage). "Value migrates from the commoditising layer" is the single most reliable tech pattern (cf. PCs: value left hardware for OS/apps).
- **P:** continued aggressive token-price deflation **through 2028: ~85%** (near-certain); whether it's net *bullish* (Jevons) or *bearish* (capex deflation) for compute is the genuine coin-flip — **~50/50**.
- **Signposts:** API price/token trends; open vs closed model share; inference-vs-training compute mix; enterprise self-hosting adoption; whether hyperscaler *revenue* per unit of capex rises or falls.
- **Book read-through:** *relative* winner is **Amazon/AWS** (neutral "arms-dealer of inference" — hosts whatever model wins, incl. cheap open ones); ambiguous for **ASML/AMD** (more total compute = good; deflated capex = bad). The Jevons-vs-deflation question is the hinge of the whole AI complex.
- **Premortem:** frontier capability stays scarce and expensive (a moat persists at the top), keeping model-layer pricing power and justifying capex.

### A5 — Capability discontinuity: an AGI-ish jump (the fat tail, both directions)
- **The story:** a model crosses a threshold that **automates a large share of knowledge work**. Two opposite market narratives: **(i) productivity boom** — a supply-side miracle, disinflationary growth, equity melt-up, the soft-landing/Goldilocks of D-cluster; or **(ii) labor/social shock** — white-collar displacement, political backlash, "tax/regulate AI," winner-take-all anxiety.
- **Outside view:** general-purpose technologies (electricity, IT) *do* eventually raise productivity, but with **long, uneven lags** (the "productivity paradox"). Discontinuous overnight jumps are rare; the base rate for "this year is the step-change" is low in any given year, but compounds over a decade.
- **P:** a clear, economy-wide productivity-acceleration narrative taking hold **by 2030: ~30–40%**; a *disruptive labor-shock* political narrative **by 2030: ~25–35%** (not mutually exclusive — both can run).
- **Signposts:** measured productivity stats; AI-agent reliability benchmarks; corporate headcount vs output; labor-market data in exposed white-collar sectors; political/legislative reaction (AI taxes, UBI talk).
- **Book read-through:** productivity-boom = broad equity tailwind (esp. ASML/AMD/Amazon, and margin expansion everywhere); labor-shock = volatility, regulatory de-rating of AI leaders, defensive bid (staples, utilities). Either way, **knowledge-work-heavy** businesses (the banks' cost base, Zalando's ops) see margin upside.
- **Premortem:** capability plateaus (scaling hits diminishing returns); reliability/agentic execution stays too poor for real automation; deployment friction (regulation, trust, integration) caps impact for years.

### A6 — The agentic economy: autonomous AI agents become economic actors
- **The story:** AI agents **transact autonomously** — shopping, booking, negotiating, allocating. Changes the front-end of commerce (who/what is the "customer"), advertising (agents don't watch ads), payments, and search. "Agent-first" commerce reshapes e-commerce economics.
- **Outside view:** intermediation layers reorder every few decades (branch → web → mobile → ?). New interface paradigms do shift rents, but adoption is slower than hype.
- **P:** agentic commerce as a *material* channel **by 2030: ~35–45%**.
- **Signposts:** agent-checkout volumes; retailer "agent API" adoption; ad-model disruption signals; payment-rail changes for agents.
- **Book read-through:** **Amazon** (could own the agent layer *or* be disintermediated by it); **Zalando** (curation/marketplace either amplified or bypassed — see its [narrative](../05_narratives/ZALANDO_narrative.md)); advertising-dependent models at risk.
- **Premortem:** trust/liability/regulation keep humans in the loop for real spending; agents stay assistive, not autonomous.

### A7 — The AI safety/security clampdown: an incident triggers heavy regulation
- **The story:** a high-profile AI-caused harm — a cyber/bio uplift event, a market-moving deepfake, an autonomous-agent failure, an election incident — triggers **emergency regulation**: licensing, mandatory KYC everywhere (the Fable-ban/Persona-ID model generalises — see [AI_STACK_WARS §1](AI_STACK_WARS.md)), compute caps, liability regimes.
- **Outside view:** transformative technologies get their "Three Mile Island / Thalidomide" moment that hardwires regulation. The base rate of *eventually* is high; *timing* is the uncertainty.
- **P:** a significant AI-safety incident driving new binding regulation **by 2030: ~55%**.
- **Signposts:** near-miss incidents; legislative momentum (EU AI Act enforcement, US executive action); insurer behaviour; KYC/identity mandates spreading.
- **Book read-through:** raises compliance costs + policy-beta across AI names; *favours incumbents* with compliance scale (Amazon, large clouds) over open/long-tail; could *cap* the open-source/China-stack narrative or *accelerate* the bifurcation (B2).
- **Premortem:** AI proves boringly safe in practice; regulation stays light-touch/pro-innovation (the current US tilt).

# Cluster B — The chip & geopolitics axis

### B1 — The Taiwan / TSMC supply shock *(the $10 trillion tail)*
- **The story:** a blockade or conflict around Taiwan disrupts **TSMC (~90% of leading-edge chips, ~70% made in Taiwan; CoWoS packaging booked out through 2026)**. Bloomberg estimates a Taiwan conflict at **~$10tn / ~10% of world GDP** — larger than COVID. The **PLA is targeting 2027 readiness**; TSMC Arizona 2nm isn't volume until **~2030**, so there's no near-term mitigation ([longyield](https://longyield.substack.com/p/the-taiwan-semiconductor-risk-the), [AEI](https://www.aei.org/articles/how-disruptive-would-a-chinese-invasion-of-taiwan-be/)).
- **Outside view:** great-power chokepoint crises are **rare in any given year** (deterrence, MAD-of-chips, mutual economic destruction), but tail-heavy. Forecasters (Swift Centre, etc.) put a full invasion this decade in the low-but-not-trivial range; a *blockade/coercion* episode is more likely than outright invasion.
- **P:** a *market-disrupting* Taiwan-Strait episode (blockade, quarantine, major incident — not necessarily full war) **by 2030: ~20–30%**; full invasion **by 2030: ~10–15%**.
- **Signposts:** PLA exercise tempo/scale; US-China rhetoric & chip-policy escalation; Taiwan election politics; TSMC ex-Taiwan diversification pace; insurance/shipping risk premia in the Strait.
- **Book read-through:** **catastrophic and book-wide** — ASML (customers' fabs offline), AMD (no TSMC = no MI-series), Amazon (no chips for AWS), Melexis (auto-chip supply); a global risk-off + inflation spike (chip scarcity) that also hits banks/cyclicals. The ultimate "everything" tail. Partial hedge: ex-Taiwan capacity (Arizona, Japan, Europe) and ASML's structural role in *rebuilding* capacity.
- **Premortem:** deterrence holds (the "silicon shield" works precisely because the chips are too valuable to break); diversification (Arizona/Japan) de-risks over time; China's own dependence on the same supply chain stays its hand.

### B2 — Full US–China tech bifurcation (two stacks, two internets)
- **The story:** export controls + China's domestic success (**GLM-5.2 trained on Huawei Ascend, zero Nvidia** — [AI_STACK_WARS §6](AI_STACK_WARS.md)) harden into **two parallel, non-interoperable tech spheres** — chips, models, standards, payment rails. The world picks sides.
- **Outside view:** bifurcation/decoupling of strategic tech between rivals has clear historical analogues (Cold War tech blocs); once started it tends to ratchet, not reverse.
- **P:** material, durable bifurcation deepening **by 2030: ~60%**.
- **Signposts:** export-control scope; Huawei/SMIC capacity & yield; China domestic-model share; "tech bloc" alignment of third countries; ASML DUV-to-China policy.
- **Book read-through:** **ASML** (China TAM capped, but DUV leverage over SMIC; ex-China fab build-out offsets), **AMD/Melexis** (China share lost). A *negotiated easing* is the upside surprise. Extends **S13**.
- **Premortem:** a grand bargain / trade deal (B-cluster détente); mutual economic dependence forces pragmatic interoperability.

### B3 — Deglobalization / friend-shoring entrenchment
- **The story:** tariffs, industrial policy, and supply-chain regionalisation become permanent. "In-region, for-region" manufacturing; resilience over efficiency; structurally higher goods inflation.
- **Outside view:** globalization has ebbed and flowed; the post-2018 reversal has policy momentum across both US parties and the EU. Trend-following base rate favours continuation.
- **P:** entrenchment continuing **through 2030: ~70%**.
- **Signposts:** tariff actions; reshoring capex; inventory/"just-in-case" levels; FDI flows; content-localisation rules.
- **Book read-through:** **ABB** (a reshoring *winner* — 80% US-made; electrical kit for re-industrialisation), **Melexis** ("China Speed" localisation), **Unilever/Carlsberg** (input-cost & supply-chain pressure), banks (trade-finance shifts). Structurally **inflationary → supports the rate axis** (banks over utilities).
- **Premortem:** a productivity boom (A5) makes cheap-labor arbitrage irrelevant and re-globalises via automation; recession forces a return to cost-minimisation.

# Cluster C — The monetary & fiscal regime

### C1 — Fiscal dominance & financial repression
- **The story:** sovereign debt loads (US deficits, defense + AI + energy + aging spending) grow so large that **monetary policy becomes subordinate to financing the state** — central banks tolerate higher inflation, cap yields (explicit or implicit YCC), and **financial repression** (negative real rates) erodes the debt. Hard assets (**gold, Bitcoin, real assets**) bid; "**confidence in America**" / the dollar's reserve status is questioned.
- **Outside view:** this is the *historical norm* for how heavily-indebted great powers resolve debt (post-WWII US/UK, 1940s–50s repression). Base rate of "inflate/repress away the debt" >> "austerity" or "default."
- **P:** financial-repression dynamics becoming a *dominant* market narrative **by 2030: ~45–55%**.
- **Signposts:** debt/GDP & interest-expense trajectory; real yields; gold & Bitcoin behaviour vs real rates; central-bank balance-sheet & forward-guidance shifts; term-premium repricing; foreign Treasury demand.
- **Book read-through:** **bullish hard assets & banks** (steeper curves, nominal-asset inflation — BNP/ACA), **bearish long-duration** (utilities' discount rate; high-multiple growth — ASML/AMD/Amazon multiples) unless growth offsets. The cross-cuts: feeds A1 (state-funded AI), C3, and a weaker dollar.
- **Premortem:** AI productivity boom (A5) grows the economy out of the debt (the optimistic exit); fiscal discipline returns under market pressure (a bond-vigilante moment forces austerity instead).

### C2 — The inflation-volatility regime (vs the disinflation alternative)
- **The story (base case-ish):** structurally **higher and choppier inflation** — driven by deglobalization (B3), the energy transition + energy-security spending (D1), demographics/labor scarcity (E2), fiscal largesse (C1), and AI-power demand. Central banks fight a recurring battle; **rates stay higher-for-longer with bigger swings** (the 2026 oil-shock hike — [ECB_RATE_REGIME](ECB_RATE_REGIME.md) — is a preview). **The disinflation fork:** AI productivity + cheap energy + China deflation export the *opposite* — soft landing, falling rates.
- **Outside view:** the post-1990 "Great Moderation" was historically *unusual*; a return to more volatile inflation is a regression to a longer-run mean. But disinflationary tech/demography forces are also real (Japanification risk).
- **P:** a higher-vol inflation regime dominating **2026–2030: ~55%**; the clean-disinflation soft-landing **~30%**; outright deflation-scare **~15%**.
- **Signposts:** core services inflation stickiness; wage growth; inflation-expectation surveys/breakevens; energy prices; the *amplitude* of rate cycles.
- **Book read-through:** higher-vol = **banks win (NII), utilities & long-duration growth lose** (the rate-axis pair — [scenario tree](MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md)); disinflation = the mirror (utilities + growth re-rate, the S1 relief).
- **Premortem:** AI/energy deflation dominates; a hard recession resets inflation; central banks credibly re-anchor.

### C3 — Fed-independence crisis / "Sell America"
- **The story:** the **Warsh Fed** under political pressure (Trump installed him to cut — [AI_STACK_WARS §8](AI_STACK_WARS.md)) loses credibility; markets price a **politicised central bank** → higher term premium, weaker dollar, a "sell US assets" rotation toward gold, ex-US equities, and (ironically) European banks.
- **Outside view:** central-bank-independence erosion episodes (1970s US, EM histories) reliably produce currency/term-premium repricing. Institutions are sticky, though — full capture is rarer than feared.
- **P:** a *credibility-questioning* episode driving a dollar/term-premium repricing **by 2028: ~30–40%**; a full-blown independence crisis **~15%**.
- **Signposts:** the gap between Warsh's *words* (hawkish) and *actions*; political interference headlines; long-end yields & term premium; DXY; gold; foreign demand for Treasuries.
- **Book read-through:** weak-dollar + ex-US rotation = relative tailwind for **European banks (BNP/ACA)** and EUR earners; gold bid; US mega-cap multiples (Amazon/AMD) pressured by higher US term premium. Feeds C1.
- **Premortem:** Warsh over-delivers independence to build credibility (the "credibility theatre" that's actually *real* hawkishness); institutions hold.

# Cluster D — Energy & climate

### D1 — Energy as the master variable
- **The story:** energy security trumps the transition; oil/gas get **weaponised** in recurring geopolitical cycles (the 2026 Iran/Hormuz shock — [ECB_RATE_REGIME](ECB_RATE_REGIME.md) — as the template); the green build-out *adds* to demand (electrification + AI power) rather than replacing hydrocarbons fast. **Energy is the upstream driver of inflation → rates → everything.**
- **Outside view:** energy shocks have triggered most post-war recessions and inflation spikes. Energy as "the master variable" is a high base-rate claim.
- **P:** at least one more **major energy-price shock by 2030: ~70%**; energy staying a top-3 macro driver throughout: **~85%**.
- **Signposts:** Brent & gas; Middle East / Russia / Strait risk; spare capacity (OPEC+); electricity prices; transition-vs-security policy swings.
- **Book read-through:** input-cost hit to **Unilever/Carlsberg/ABB**; inflation → rates → **banks up / utilities & growth multiples down**; a structural bid under **power/grid (Iberdrola/Enel/ABB)** via electrification. Energy is the connective tissue across C2, D2, A2.
- **Premortem:** abundant cheap energy (solar+storage cost collapse, a gas glut, fusion surprise) breaks the link and is *disinflationary*.

### D2 — The nuclear / SMR renaissance (the A2 sub-plot, as its own narrative)
- **The story:** AI power demand + decarbonisation + energy security revive **nuclear** — restarts (Three Mile Island), life-extensions, and the first commercial **SMRs**; "**24/7 clean firm power**" becomes the prize hyperscalers pay up for (≥5GW dedicated nuclear for data centres by 2030).
- **Outside view:** nuclear renaissances have been *announced* repeatedly and underdelivered (cost overruns, timelines, politics). Healthy skepticism warranted — but the *demand pull* (hyperscaler willingness to pay) is genuinely new.
- **P:** a real, sustained nuclear/SMR investment narrative **by 2030: ~60%**; SMRs at *material* scale **by 2032: ~30%** (execution risk).
- **Signposts:** SMR orders/approvals; restart announcements; hyperscaler nuclear PPAs; regulatory (NRC) reform; cost/timeline track record.
- **Book read-through:** indirect for the book (no pure-play nuclear name), but supports the **Iberdrola/Enel/ABB** electrification thesis and the broader "power is the bottleneck" (A2) re-rate.
- **Premortem:** cost/time overruns; renewables+storage out-compete on price; political backlash; the AI demand that justifies it busts (A3).

### D3 — Climate physical-risk repricing
- **The story:** a cluster of extreme-weather/insurance events forces markets to **price physical climate risk** — insurer retreat from regions, stranded assets, adaptation capex, migration/agricultural shocks.
- **Outside view:** slow-moving but monotonic; markets chronically under-price tail risks until a salient event re-rates them suddenly.
- **P:** a *market-relevant* climate-risk repricing event **by 2030: ~40%**.
- **Signposts:** insurance availability/pricing; catastrophe-bond spreads; regulatory disclosure regimes; agricultural/commodity shocks.
- **Book read-through:** mostly indirect; supports grid-hardening/electrification capex (ABB/utilities); input/agri cost risk for **Carlsberg/Unilever**; regulatory cost for all.
- **Premortem:** adaptation + technology absorb it gradually with no repricing "moment"; political deprioritisation.

# Cluster E — Society, demographics, consumption

### E1 — GLP-1 permanently reshapes consumption
- **The story:** obesity/diabetes drugs (semaglutide, tirzepatide, next-gen amycretin) scale globally and **structurally reduce consumption** of calories, alcohol, snacks — and lift health/longevity. A multi-year demand-destruction overhang on food/drink; a multi-year tailwind for the drug-makers (if they execute).
- **Outside view:** genuinely new — few clean analogues for a pharmacological shift in mass consumption. But adoption-curve base rates for transformative drugs (statins) suggest slow, large, durable penetration.
- **P:** measurable, sustained consumption impact on food/alcohol **by 2030: ~55–65%**.
- **Signposts:** GLP-1 prescription penetration; category volume data (snacks, alcohol, soft drinks); reformulation trends; pricing/access (the Trump MFN deal); real-world consumption studies.
- **Book read-through:** structural bear on **Unilever** (de-risking by exiting Foods → McCormick — see its [narrative](../05_narratives/UNILEVER_narrative.md)) and **Carlsberg** (so far "no significant impact" — the key tell to watch); the *upside* leg is **Novo Nordisk** *if* amycretin/oral execute (see its [narrative](../05_narratives/NOVO_NORDISK_narrative.md)).
- **Premortem:** tolerability/cost/access cap penetration; consumers compensate (smaller but premium); the effect plateaus.

### E2 — The demographic cliff
- **The story:** aging + shrinking workforces (Europe, China, Japan, then more) → **labor scarcity, healthcare/pension demand, fiscal strain, structurally lower trend growth** — but also wage support and (debated) inflation pressure. China's demographic turn compounds its property/consumer malaise.
- **Outside view:** demographics are the most *forecastable* macro variable (the people are already born). The direction is near-certain; the market *timing* is the uncertainty.
- **P:** demographics as a recognised dominant driver of growth/fiscal narratives **by 2030: ~70%**.
- **Signposts:** working-age population trends; dependency ratios; labor-force participation; healthcare/pension spending; immigration policy.
- **Book read-through:** supports **Novo/healthcare** demand; pressures **China-exposed** names (Kone, Carlsberg); labor scarcity favours **automation (ABB)** and is a tailwind for the AI-productivity story (A5); fiscal strain feeds C1.
- **Premortem:** AI/automation offsets labor scarcity (productivity solves it); immigration or pro-natal policy shifts the curve; longer working lives.

### E3 — China: reflation vs Japanification
- **The story (the fork):** either China **stimulates out** of the property bust (the "two new" + treasury-bond modernisation push — see [Kone narrative](../05_narratives/KONE_narrative.md) — broadens into real reflation, lifting the consumer) **OR** China enters a **Japan-style balance-sheet recession** — years of deleveraging, weak confidence, deflation export.
- **Outside view:** post-property-bubble economies (Japan 1990s, others) more often grind through long balance-sheet recessions than V-shape out — the base rate tilts toward Japanification absent forceful, sustained stimulus.
- **P:** durable Chinese *reflation* **by 2028: ~30–40%**; Japanification/long-grind the larger probability **~50–60%**.
- **Signposts:** China property prices/starts; credit impulse; consumer confidence & savings rate; stimulus scale/follow-through; CNY; youth unemployment.
- **Book read-through:** reflation = upside for **Kone** (property + modernisation), **Carlsberg** (beer/consumer), **ASML/Melexis** (China chip demand), **Unilever** (EM); Japanification = the opposite + a global-disinflation export. The single biggest swing for the China-cluster names.
- **Premortem:** Beijing finally goes "whatever it takes" on the consumer; or external demand (exports) bails it out.

# Cluster F — The financial system

### F1 — Private-credit / shadow-bank blow-up
- **The story:** the explosive growth of **private credit** (incl. ~$200bn+ into AI infra — see A3) hides leverage and maturity/liquidity mismatches; a cycle turn triggers a **shadow-bank stress event** that spills into banks (counterparty, sentiment) and the real economy. The ECB has explicitly flagged private-markets exposure (see [BNP narrative](../05_narratives/BNP_PARIBAS_narrative.md)).
- **Outside view:** rapid credit expansion into an under-regulated channel late in a cycle is a *classic* pre-crisis setup (S&Ls, subprime/SIVs, CLOs). High base rate of *eventual* stress; timing uncertain.
- **P:** a *named* private-credit stress event **by 2030: ~35–45%**; systemic spillover **~15–20%**.
- **Signposts:** private-credit AUM growth & mark-to-market behaviour; NAV-lending & PIK-interest trends; default/recovery rates; bank exposure disclosures; AI-infra loan performance (the overlap with A3).
- **Book read-through:** **banks (BNP, ACA, Banca Mediolanum)** — direct exposure + the "is this 2008 again" sentiment hit; risk-off everywhere. Ironically, BNP is *leaning into* private assets via AXA IM Alts — both a growth story and a risk flag.
- **Premortem:** private credit proves resilient (long lock-ups, no run dynamics, patient capital); the cycle stays benign; losses are absorbed quietly.

### F2 — A European revival — or fragmentation
- **The story (two-sided):** **Bull:** war/energy/AI/defense pressure forces **European integration** — joint debt, defense spending, a capital-markets union, energy independence → a structural **re-rating of European equities** (cheap vs US). **Bear:** the opposite — fiscal/political fragmentation (French/Italian debt, populism) → a sovereign-spread crisis.
- **Outside view:** European integration historically advances *through crises* ("Europe is forged in crises" — Monnet), but slowly and incompletely; fragmentation scares recur (2012) but have so far been contained by the ECB.
- **P:** a *European-revival* re-rating narrative gaining traction **by 2030: ~35%**; a *fragmentation/sovereign-stress* scare **by 2030: ~30%** (not mutually exclusive over time).
- **Signposts:** joint EU debt issuance; defense-spending trajectory; capital-markets-union progress; OAT/BTP-Bund spreads; election outcomes (France, Italy, Germany).
- **Book read-through:** the book is **~14/16 European** — a European re-rating lifts *everything* (esp. banks BNP/ACA/BMED and domestic names); fragmentation hits banks (sovereign spreads) and Italian names (Enel, BMED) hardest. This is a portfolio-level beta, not a single-name call.
- **Premortem:** muddle-through (the base case) — neither dramatic integration nor crisis, just slow grind.

## Part 2 — Synthesis: how to actually use this

### The probability × impact map (my rough ranking)
- **High-probability, high-impact (own these as base case):** A2 power bottleneck, A4 token deflation, B3 deglobalization, C2 inflation-vol regime, D1 energy master-variable, E1 GLP-1, E2 demographics.
- **Medium-probability, very-high-impact (the ones that *decide* the book):** A1 sovereign AI, A3 AI financing accident, C1 fiscal dominance, E3 China fork.
- **Lower-probability, extreme-impact (the tails to hedge/monitor, not predict):** B1 Taiwan, A5 capability discontinuity, C3 Fed crisis, F1 private-credit blow-up.

### ★ The chains matter more than any single narrative
The dangerous (and lucrative) moves are **correlated cascades**, not isolated stories:
- **The AI-unwind chain:** A4 (token deflation) → A3 (financing accident) → F1 (private-credit spillover) → risk-off → C-cluster policy response. *This is the book's biggest latent fragility (ASML/AMD/Amazon/ABB/utilities all in one chain).*
- **The geopolitical-inflation chain:** D1 (energy shock) or B1 (Taiwan) → C2 (inflation spike) → C1/C3 (fiscal/Fed strain) → rate-axis repricing.
- **The benign chain:** A5 (productivity boom) → C2-disinflation fork → A2 power demand met → broad melt-up. The bull case requires *several* things to go right.

### The monthly signpost dashboard (what I'd actually watch)
1. **Hyperscaler capex guides** (Amazon first) + **AI-credit spreads** → A3/A4 (the AI hinge).
2. **Power/PPA prices, transformer lead times, nuclear/SMR orders** → A2/D2.
3. **Oil/Brent + Middle East/Strait + Taiwan-Strait tempo** → D1/B1 (the geopolitical tails).
4. **Real yields, term premium, gold/Bitcoin, DXY** → C1/C2/C3 (the monetary regime).
5. **China property + credit impulse + consumer** → E3 (the China fork).
6. **GLP-1 penetration + category volumes** → E1.
7. **Sovereign-AI policy headlines + export controls** → A1/B2.

### The discipline (keeping myself honest, à la Tetlock)
- **Write the prior before the news** (the [recap](../04_recaps/README.md) loop). When a signpost fires, *update incrementally* — don't flip.
- **Score myself.** Revisit these probabilities quarterly; was I directionally right; were the round numbers a crutch; which base rate did I neglect?
- **Distrust the vivid.** The most *available/dramatic* narrative (Taiwan war, AGI) is usually *over*-weighted by the crowd vs the boring high-probability grind (deglobalization, demographics, token deflation). Edge often lives in the boring ones.
- **The story ≠ the trade.** For each, the question is the gap between this probability and *what's priced* — that's where the [`NARRATIVE_ENGINE`](../00_system/NARRATIVE_ENGINE.md) output (`stock — range — reason — follow-up`) takes over.
