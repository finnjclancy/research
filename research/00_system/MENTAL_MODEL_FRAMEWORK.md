# The Mental Model — Everything I Need to Know

> The operating system for the job. Three layers, top-down (World → Sector → Company), then the synthesis that turns it all into a number: **News → Range**.
>
> Re-read the principle in [MANDATE.md](MANDATE.md) before using this: **a stock only moves on the gap between what happens and what was priced in.** Every checklist below exists to answer one of three questions — *What's priced in? What changes it? How sensitive is the stock to that change?*

---

## How to use this document

- It is a **checklist of what to know**, not a one-time essay. For each name in [COVERAGE_LIST.md](COVERAGE_LIST.md) you should be able to answer most of Layer 3 from memory.
- Build it **once per layer, refresh on a cadence**: World (daily skim, weekly deep), Sector (weekly), Company (own it cold; refresh each earnings + on news).
- The output of the whole stack is the **"reflex"**: see a headline, know the range. Section 4 is how you train it.

# LAYER 1 — A Model of the World (the macro regime)

> Goal: in one paragraph, state *the pivotal story moving the market right now* and the 3–4 smaller narratives underneath it. Everything downstream inherits from this.

### 1.1 The pivotal question — "What is the one thing the market is trading on?"
Rotate through the candidates and rank them by what's actually driving price *today*:
- **Rates** — level, and more importantly the *direction of the path* and what's priced into the curve (Fed, ECB, BoJ, BoE). Real yields. Term premium.
- **Inflation** — headline vs core, sticky services, wages, expectations (breakevens). The *direction of surprise* vs consensus.
- **Growth** — soft vs hard landing, PMIs, labour market, earnings breadth. US vs Europe vs China divergence.
- **Geopolitics** — wars (Ukraine, Middle East/Iran), elections, tariffs/trade, sanctions, Taiwan.
- **Oil & commodities** — Brent level and the *risk premium*, gas, copper, gold.
- **Liquidity** — central bank balance sheets (QT/QE), credit creation, money supply, fiscal impulse.
- **Credit spreads** — IG and HY; the market's "fear gauge" for the real economy. Widening = risk-off coming.
- **The USD / DXY** — drives EM, commodities, EU exporters' translation, global financial conditions.
- **Confidence in America** — Treasury demand, dollar reserve status, debt/deficit, "sell America" trades, gold as the alternative.
- **AI** — is the market paying for AI *revenue*, AI *capex*, or AI *productivity*? Where are we in the hype/disillusionment cycle?
- **Sentiment/positioning** — risk-on vs risk-off, VIX, fund flows, where the crowded trades are.

### 1.2 The regime label
Pin a label — it tells you which factors lead and which lag:
- Goldilocks / reflation / stagflation / deflation-scare / risk-off / late-cycle / soft-landing.
- **Is it a "good news is good" or "good news is bad" tape?** (i.e. does strong data help stocks or hurt them via rates?) This single distinction flips how you read every data point.

### 1.3 The calendar (the macro catalyst clock)
Know what's coming and what's consensus for each:
- **Central banks:** FOMC, ECB, BoE, BoJ dates; the *dots*, the press conference tone, the balance-sheet plan.
- **Data:** US CPI/PCE, NFP/jobs, ISM/PMIs; EU CPI, German IFO; China PMIs, credit data.
- **Politics:** elections, budget/fiscal events, debt-ceiling, tariff deadlines, OPEC+ meetings.
- **Earnings season** start/end and the bellwethers that set the tone.

### 1.4 The cross-asset dashboard (what to glance at every morning)
- Equities: S&P, Stoxx 600, Nasdaq, Nikkei/Hang Seng + sector leadership.
- Rates: US 2y/10y, Bund 10y, curve shape (2s10s).
- FX: DXY, EUR/USD, USD/JPY, USD/CNY.
- Commodities: Brent, gold, copper.
- Credit: IG/HY spreads (or proxies like CDX/iTraxx).
- Vol: VIX, MOVE (bond vol).
> The point isn't the levels — it's **what's moving and why, and whether it confirms or breaks the prevailing narrative.**

### 1.5 The narrative-shift radar (Layer 1)
A world-narrative is shifting when: a central bank changes language; a key data series flips the direction of surprise; a cross-asset correlation breaks (e.g. stocks and bonds selling off together); a "consensus" trade starts failing to respond to supportive news. **Write down what would have to happen for the current regime label to be wrong.**

# LAYER 2 — A Model of Each Sector

> Repeat the Layer-1 questions one level down. For each sector in the book (Banks, Utilities, Staples, Semis/Tech, Industrials, Health Care, Consumer Discretionary, Comm Services/Gaming) build this once and refresh weekly.

### 2.1 The sector's pivotal narrative + the smaller ones
- What is the *one story* the market tells about this sector right now? And the 3–4 sub-narratives under it.
- Is the sector **in or out of favour** (flows, relative performance vs index)? Why?

### 2.2 The sector's structural drivers (the dials that move every name in it)
- **The master variable.** Most sectors have one dominant macro linkage — name it:
  - Banks → *rates/NIM, loan growth, credit quality, regulation, capital return.*
  - Utilities → *bond yields (discount rate), power prices, regulated returns/RAB, renewables capex, debt cost.*
  - Staples → *pricing vs volume, input costs, EM/FX, private-label pressure, GLP-1 demand effects.*
  - Semis → *the cycle (inventory), AI capex, China/export controls, end-market mix (AI vs PC vs auto vs industrial).*
  - Industrials → *PMIs/short vs long cycle, electrification, construction, orders/book-to-bill, China.*
  - Health Care → *pipeline/clinical data, pricing/policy (US IRA), patent cliffs, volume/supply.*
  - Discretionary → *consumer health, real incomes, e-commerce penetration, margins/promotions.*
  - Gaming/Comm → *release slate, engagement, ad cycle, content costs, M&A/breakups.*
- **Cycle position:** early/mid/late cycle, inventory cycle, capex cycle.
- **Supply/demand balance** specific to the sector (e.g. semis wafer capacity, utility power supply, beer volumes).

### 2.3 The competitive map
- Who are the leaders, challengers, disruptors? **Market share** and the direction it's moving.
- **Pricing power / moat:** consolidated vs fragmented, switching costs, regulation as a barrier.
- **Read-throughs:** when peer X reports or pre-announces, what does it imply for my name? (Build a peer cross-reference — e.g. ASML reads from TSMC/Intel/Samsung capex; AMD from Nvidia; banks from each other.)
- Substitutes and adjacent threats (e.g. GLP-1s hitting food/alcohol volumes — links Novo → Carlsberg/Unilever).

### 2.4 The sector calendar & data
- Industry data series (e.g. SEMI billings, auto SAAR, beer volume data, bank lending surveys, power prices).
- Sector conferences, capital-markets days, regulatory decision dates.
- The peer earnings sequence — who reports first and sets the read.

### 2.5 Valuation framework for the sector
- **What multiple does the market use** for this sector and why (P/E, EV/EBITDA, P/B & ROTE for banks, EV/EBIT, DCF for utilities, P/Sales for hyper-growth)?
- The historical range of that multiple and where it sits now (cheap/expensive vs own history and vs peers).
- What the multiple is *most sensitive to* (rates for utilities/long-duration; ROTE for banks; growth durability for semis).

### 2.6 Sector narrative-shift radar
- A sector re-rates when: the master variable inflects (rates peak → utilities/REITs bid); the cycle turns (inventory bottoms → semis); a regulatory regime changes; flows rotate (value↔growth, defensives↔cyclicals). **Know the leading indicator for each.**

# LAYER 3 — A Model of Each Company

> This is where the money is. For every name you should own all of the below cold. Each company gets its own file in `03_companies/`. The structure below is the template.

### 3.1 The one-paragraph thesis & the narrative
- **What is the story the market believes about this stock right now?** (the bull case and the bear case in two sentences each).
- **Why do people own it / short it?** What is it *for* in a portfolio (compounder, cyclical, turnaround, deep value, GARP, dividend)?
- **What is consensus, and what's my variant view (if any)?**

### 3.2 The business — what it actually does
- Revenue by **segment, geography, product** (and which segment the market actually cares about — often one segment drives the whole stock, e.g. AWS for Amazon, EUV/High-NA for ASML, GLP-1 for Novo, data-center for AMD).
- The unit economics: what drives volume, what drives price, what drives mix.
- **Total growth vs organic growth** — strip out M&A and FX to see the real engine. The market rewards organic.
- Customers, concentration, contract structure (backlog/orders/book-to-bill where relevant).

### 3.3 The income statement — the lines that matter
> Know which 3–4 lines the stock actually trades on. For each, know consensus and the recent trend.
- **Top line** — growth rate, organic vs reported, FX impact, pricing vs volume split.
- **Gross margin** — input costs, pricing power, mix.
- **Operating margin / EBIT** — operating leverage, cost programs, one-offs.
- **Below the line** — net interest (matters more with leverage), tax rate.
- **EPS** — and how buybacks flatter it (EPS growth ≠ net income growth).
- **The KPI the market fixates on** that isn't in GAAP (e.g. ASML bookings, Novo prescription/script trends, bank NII & cost-of-risk, Zalando GMV & active customers, AMD data-center revenue, Carlsberg organic volume).
- **Guidance** — what management guided, the credibility of that guide, and the *direction of revisions* (rising or falling estimates is one of the most powerful return predictors).

### 3.4 Balance sheet, cash flow & capital allocation
- **Net debt / leverage**, maturities, cost of debt (huge for utilities & leveraged names in a rate move).
- **Free cash flow** conversion — the truth serum vs accounting earnings.
- **Capital allocation:** dividend (level, cover, policy), **buybacks** (size, is it accretive, is it propping the stock), **capex** (esp. AI capex — is it investment or value destruction?), **M&A** appetite.
- **Debt issuance / equity issuance** signals — raising equity is usually a negative surprise; refinancing risk in a high-rate world.

### 3.5 Management & governance
- CEO/CFO — track record, credibility, how the market trusts (or doesn't) their guidance.
- Capital-allocation history (do they create or destroy value with M&A/buybacks?).
- Incentive structure, insider ownership, recent insider buying/selling.
- Communication style — do they sandbag guidance (then beat) or over-promise?
- Key-person / strategy risk, succession, activist presence.

### 3.6 Legal, regulatory & ESG status
- Litigation, antitrust, investigations, sanctions exposure.
- Regulatory regime (banks: capital/Basel/stress tests; utilities: regulated returns/price caps; pharma: approvals, pricing/IRA; tech: export controls, antitrust, DMA/DSA).
- ESG/controversy flags that can drive flows (exclusions) or costs (carbon).

### 3.7 What moves the stock — the sensitivity map (the heart of it)
> For each name, list its **return drivers ranked by impact**, with a rough beta/elasticity to each:
- Macro sensitivities: rates (bp move → % stock), FX (which crosses), oil/input cost, China, US consumer.
- Sector sensitivities: the master variable from Layer 2.
- Idiosyncratic drivers: the one or two KPIs / events that dominate (e.g. a clinical readout, a bookings number, a capacity announcement, a content release).
- **Operating leverage & financial leverage** — how a small revenue surprise amplifies into EPS.
- **Multiple sensitivity** — is this a "rate-duration" stock (re-rates on yields) or an "earnings" stock (re-rates on estimates)?

### 3.8 What's priced in — consensus & positioning
- **Consensus estimates** (revenue/EBIT/EPS for current & next year) and the **trend of revisions**.
- **Sell-side:** distribution of ratings, the bull/bear price targets, who the influential analyst is, recent upgrades/downgrades and *why*.
- **Valuation:** current multiple vs own history, vs peers, vs the market. What growth/margin is implied.
- **Positioning:** is it a crowded long or a crowded short? Short interest, fund ownership, hedge-fund favourite? (Crowded positioning makes the move *bigger* when surprised — the unwind dominates.)
- **The whisper number** — what the buy-side really expects vs the published consensus.

### 3.9 The catalyst calendar (per company)
- Earnings dates, capital-markets days, product launches, clinical readouts, regulatory decisions, contract renewals, index rebalances, lock-up expiries.
- For each catalyst: **what's expected, what's the bull/bear outcome, and the rough move in each case.**

### 3.10 How to spot a narrative shift (per company)
- The leading tells: estimate revisions turning, the stock failing to respond to good news (exhausted bulls) or shrugging off bad news (washed-out bears), a new KPI the market starts quoting, sell-side re-framing the story, management changing the language.
- **Write the "narrative break" scenario explicitly:** "The bull story dies if ___; the bear story dies if ___." That sentence is where the biggest re-ratings come from.

# SECTION 4 — The Synthesis: News → Range (the reflex)

> This is the actual job from [MANDATE.md](MANDATE.md). Sections 1–3 are the inputs; this is the function that maps a headline to a number.

### 4.1 The standing pre-work (do this *before* the news, for every name)
For each stock, keep a one-pager that has, current at all times:
1. **What's priced in** (consensus, valuation, positioning) — §3.8
2. **The catalyst calendar with expected outcomes** — §3.9
3. **The sensitivity map** — §3.7
4. **The bull/bear narrative + the break conditions** — §3.10

If those four are current, you can react instantly. If they're stale, you're guessing.

### 4.2 The reaction algorithm (when news hits)
1. **Classify the news:** which driver does it touch (top line / margin / KPI / capital allocation / regulatory / macro read-through / management)?
2. **Surprise vs expectations:** is it better/worse than what was priced in, and by how much? (No surprise = no move, *even if the news is "good."*)
3. **Estimate the earnings impact:** does it change this year / next year EPS or just sentiment? Translate the surprise into a revision (%).
4. **Estimate the multiple impact:** does it change the *narrative* (re-rate the multiple) or just the number? Narrative-changers move multiples and are far bigger.
5. **Apply positioning:** crowded long + bad news = amplified; washed-out short + good news = amplified.
6. **Output a range:** `move ≈ (earnings revision %) + (multiple change %)`, then widen/narrow for positioning and liquidity. Give it as a **range with a base/bull/bear**, not a point.
7. **Sanity-check vs history:** how has this stock moved on similar news before? (Earnings-day historical moves, prior shocks.) The market gives you comparables — use them.

### 4.3 The output format (what an answer looks like)
> "On [news], because consensus had [X priced in] and this implies [revision/narrative change], I'd expect [base case +Y%], range [bear +A% to bull +B%], with the move skewed [direction] because positioning is [crowded/clean]. The key swing factor is [Z]."

### 4.4 Calibration loop (how the reflex gets good)
- **Log every prediction** before the move, then mark it to market. Where you were wrong, was it the *direction*, the *magnitude*, or the *positioning*?
- Build a memory of **earnings-day moves** and **shock responses** per name.
- Update the one-pagers after every print so "what's priced in" never goes stale.

# SECTION 5 — The "other things too" (don't forget these)

- **Liquidity & microstructure:** float, ADV, index membership, who the marginal buyer/seller is, lock-ups, passive flows, rebalance dates.
- **Currency reporting vs trading** — many EU names report in EUR but earn in USD/EM; translation vs transaction FX.
- **Tax, accounting and one-offs** — adjusted vs reported, what's being adjusted out, restructuring charges.
- **Ownership structure** — family/state holdings (common in EU), free float, voting structures, government as shareholder (Enel/Iberdrola-type situations).
- **Dividends & ex-dates** — mechanical price effects, yield support.
- **Seasonality** — both the business and the stock (sell-in-May, January effect, sector seasonals).
- **Behavioural traps:** anchoring to your own target, confirmation bias on the narrative you like, recency bias after a big move, falling in love with a name.
- **Second-order / read-through thinking:** every fact about one name is data about its peers, suppliers, customers (the cross-reference web in §2.3).
- **The "so what / vs expectations" discipline** — the most important habit: never react to a fact in isolation, always against what was priced.
- **Know the bear case better than the bears** — you only know what's priced in if you've genuinely modelled the other side.
