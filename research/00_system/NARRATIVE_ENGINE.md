# The Narrative Engine — How perception becomes price

> The core intellectual model for the job. **Numbers are the evidence; narrative is the verdict.** A stock's price is the *story the market is currently telling itself about the future*, expressed as a multiple on a set of expectations. Your job is to know that story so precisely that when news breaks you can predict **how the market will re-weight the story → into a price-range** faster than anyone else.
>
> Companion to [`MENTAL_MODEL_FRAMEWORK.md`](MENTAL_MODEL_FRAMEWORK.md) (the *what to know*). This file is the *how perception works*. Feeds [`../02_sectors/SECTOR_NARRATIVES.md`](../02_sectors/SECTOR_NARRATIVES.md) and [`../01_macro/MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md`](../01_macro/MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md).

---

## 1. The first principle

> **Price ≠ value. Price = consensus belief about the future × the conviction with which it's held.**

A stock does not move on news. It moves on the **gap between the news and the story already priced in**, multiplied by **how many people have to change their mind** and **how violently they'll act when they do.** So three things, always:

1. **What is the story?** (the dominant narrative + the sub-narratives)
2. **How crowded / conviction-held is it?** (positioning, stage in the narrative life-cycle)
3. **What would re-weight it, and by how much?** (the catalyst × the sensitivity × the positioning amplifier)

Everything below is machinery for answering those three at the speed of an intercom call.

---

## 2. The three-layer narrative map (do this for every name)

For each stock, hold a living answer to these — at **World**, **Sector**, and **Company** level:

| Question | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **What is its place?** | Where it sits in the value chain / the world's structure. |
| **Why is it necessary?** | The reason it exists — the deeper the necessity, the more durable the story. |
| **Why would someone own it?** | The *role* in a portfolio: compounder, cyclical, turnaround, yield, optionality. |
| **What's the growth / expansion story?** | The reason the multiple is what it is — the future being paid for. |
| **The bull narrative** | The story in its best light (know it cold). |
| **The bear narrative** | The story in its worst light (**know it better than the bears** — you can't know what's priced unless you've modelled the other side). |
| **★ Which narrative shift is it most levered to?** | The single macro/sector story whose change would re-rate it most. This is the link into the scenario tree. |

> The discipline: a stock is only "interesting" when there's a **gap between the dominant narrative and a plausible alternative narrative** that the market hasn't priced. That gap is the trade.

## 3. The narrative life-cycle (the psychology of perception)

Every story moves through stages. **The same news has opposite effects at different stages** — this is the most important thing to internalise.

| Stage | Description | Ownership / valuation | How news lands |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Genesis** | A real change + an early-believer thesis. Under-owned, often cheap or ignored. | Contrarian, light | Good news barely moves it (no believers yet); the asymmetry is *up*. |
| **2. Acceptance** | Sell-side adopts, estimates rise, media notices, flows start. | Building | Good news rewarded, momentum builds. The sweet spot to own. |
| **3. Consensus / crowding** | Everyone owns it; it's the "obvious" trade; priced for the story to *continue*. | Crowded, premium | **Beats needed just to hold.** Maximum vulnerability — disappointment is violent. |
| **4. Disappointment / break** | Reality diverges from the priced story; marginal buyer exhausted; **good news stops working.** | Long unwind | Small misses → large drops. The de-rate. |
| **5. Capitulation / re-rate-down** | Story dies; becomes cheap, ignored, "value trap." | Washed out | **Bad news stops working** — the bear is exhausted; asymmetry flips *up*. |
| **6. Rehabilitation** | A new story forms; cycle restarts. | Contrarian again | Back to stage 1. |

**Where the book sits now (June 2026) — diagnostic:**
- **Stage 3 (crowded, beats-needed):** ASML, AMD, Amazon, ABB — the AI-capex/electrification longs. *Asymmetry is down; in-line is a disappointment.*
- **Stage 2 (acceptance, re-rating up):** BNP, Crédit Agricole, Banca Mediolanum — European banks on the rate story.
- **Stage 4→5 (broken / washing out):** Novo Nordisk — good news (the pill beat) starting to work again = early rehabilitation tell.
- **Stage 1→2 / out-of-favour:** Enel, Iberdrola (waiting on a dovish rate pivot), Kone (waiting on China bottom), Carlsberg (China), Melexis (auto-cycle trough).
- **Special-situation (own clock):** Embracer (breakup), Unilever (steady compounder, low-beta).

> **The reflex shortcut:** before you price any news, ask "what stage is this name in?" A beat in stage 3 can fall; a less-bad print in stage 5 can rip.

## 4. ★ The game theory of market participants (whose story is it?)

A narrative is not abstract — it is *manufactured and weighted by actors with motives.* To predict how a story will be received, know **who is pushing it, what they want, and how much credibility/flow they command.** This is the most underrated edge.

| Participant | Core motive | How they shape narrative | What to watch |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Central banks (Fed/ECB)** | Inflation anchor + employment + financial stability; above all **credibility**. | Set the discount rate for *everything*; telegraph via forward guidance — they hate surprising markets but will sacrifice growth to protect inflation credibility. | The **reaction function**. Markets front-run them. The "Fed put" exists but has an inflation strike. Tone > the decision. |
| **Politicians** | Re-election, popularity, legacy. **Short horizon** (election cycle). | Weaponise the things voters *feel*: gas prices, drug prices, jobs, the stock market as scoreboard. Intervene where there's visible pain. | Trump = transactional, uses tariffs/drug-pricing (TrumpRx) as leverage and as wins; pressures the Fed. Policy lands on Novo (pricing), oil, tariffs (ASML/Melexis/Amazon). |
| **Media** | Attention. Simple, emotional, single-variable stories. | **Amplify and crystallise** a forming narrative — rarely lead price, but accelerate perception shifts and create reflexive flow loops ("AI bubble", "Ozempic kills food", "China collapse"). | Media saturation often marks a narrative *near its turning point* (stage 3→4 or 5→6). The headline is the lagging echo of positioning. |
| **CEOs** | Share price (comp/options), credibility, empire. | **Manage the narrative**: sandbag to beat, or over-promise to pump; frame the metric they want you to watch; time disclosures, buybacks, CMDs. Every CEO is now an "AI company." | When a CEO *changes the metric they emphasise*, the story is shifting (e.g. Novo pivoting to the pill; ASML's "cannot confirm 2026"). Guidance credibility is an asset they spend. |
| **Lobbies / industry bodies** | Regulation & subsidy capture. | Shape the regulatory tail (PhRMA vs drug pricing; defence; fossil vs renewables; CHIPS/semis; EU competitiveness). | The slow-moving structural tailwind/threat behind a sector's multiple. |
| **Sell-side analysts** | Banking relationships, commissions, being right *but not too contrarian* (career risk). | Provide the **consensus you measure surprise against**; herd; ratings are sticky and lag. The marginal up/downgrade moves flow. | "Whisper" (buy-side expectation) vs published consensus — the real bar. Distribution of targets = where the debate is. |
| **Hedge funds / fast money** | Absolute return; beat peers. | **The amplifier.** Crowd into themes, position around catalysts, unwind violently. | Positioning & crowding — the bigger the consensus long, the harder the disappointment (ASML −16%, Novo's collapse). |
| **Passive / index** | None — mechanical. | Buy big-caps regardless of fundamentals → **structurally supports mega-caps and momentum**; rebalances/inclusions matter. | Flow, not fundamentals. Reinforces stage-3 crowding in mega-caps (Amazon, AMD via indices). |
| **Long-only / pensions** | Relative return, valuation, income. | The **value floor** — buy dividends/cheapness (utilities, banks), sell froth slowly. | They cap downside on cheap names and are the marginal buyer in stage 5. |
| **Retail** | Story/momentum, FOMO. | Concentrate in simple narratives (AI, AMD, Novo) — add volatility and reflexivity. | Sentiment extremes; a contrarian tell at tops/bottoms. |

> **Synthesis:** a story moves price in proportion to **(the credibility of who's telling it) × (the flow they command) × (how much it changes the consensus).** A Fed tone-shift moves everything (max credibility + flow). A media "bubble" headline at stage 3 can be the top. A CEO changing their emphasised metric is an early narrative-shift tell.

## 5. The decision-tree method (macro → sector → company)

This is the structure the [`MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md`](../01_macro/MACRO_SCENARIO_TREE.md) implements. The logic:

```
MACRO narrative-shift (the world starts telling itself a new story)
   │   [each has a rough probability + the actors driving it]
   ├── SECTOR perception path #1  (how the sector's *place/necessity/story* changes)
   │      ├── COMPANY perception path A  (which name becomes MOST RELEVANT, how its story flips, range direction)
   │      ├── COMPANY perception path B
   │      └── ...
   ├── SECTOR perception path #2
   │      └── ...
   └── ...
```

**Rules for building/reading the tree:**
1. **Start from the story, not the data.** "If the world decides X, what does that *mean* for what investors want to own?"
2. **Identify the most-relevant node.** In each branch, one or two names become *the* expression of the new story — the stock the market reaches for. Name it.
3. **Direction of perception, then magnitude.** First: does the new story make the name a must-own or a must-avoid? Then: how big, given its life-cycle stage + crowding.
4. **Reflexivity & second-order.** A macro shift changes sector flows, which change the marginal buyer of a stock — model the *flow*, not just the fundamental.
5. **Pre-compute the branches** so that when news breaks you're not deriving — you're *recognising which branch just got more probable* and reading off the pre-worked range.

## 6. ★ The output protocol — "stock — range — reason — follow-up"

The deliverable on the intercom. The formula behind the range:

> **Expected move ≈ (surprise vs what's priced) × (stock's sensitivity to that variable) × (positioning amplifier) × (narrative-stage multiplier), signed by whether it confirms or breaks the dominant narrative.**

- **Surprise** — how far the news is from consensus/whisper (0 surprise = 0 move, even if "good").
- **Sensitivity** — the stock's elasticity to that driver (from each company file's §7 sensitivity map).
- **Positioning amplifier** — crowded long disappointing / washed-out short surprising = ×1.5–3.
- **Stage multiplier** — stage 3 amplifies bad news; stage 5 amplifies good news.
- **Sign** — confirms the dominant story (with-trend, smaller) vs breaks it (regime move, larger).

**The spoken format:**
> "**[Stock]** — **[base ±X%, range +A% to +B%]** — **[the one-line reason: the surprise vs what was priced]** — **[follow-up: the swing factor / what makes it the top vs bottom of the range / what to watch next]**."

**Worked examples (illustrative, from the book's documented elasticities):**
- *"ASML — down 8–14% — bookings came €2bn light of the €5bn the Street needed and they softened 2027 language; it's a crowded stage-3 long so the unwind overshoots — skewed to the low end if any peer capex guide-down follows."* (cf. the real −16% Oct-24, −11% Jul-25.)
- *"Novo — up 6–12% — pill scripts ~2× consensus and guide raised; it's washed-out stage-5 so even an in-line would've held, a beat squeezes shorts — top of range if they also nudge US injectable share."* (cf. the real Q1-26 relief.)
- *"BNP — up 2–5% — ECB hike + NII beat with cost of risk still <40bp; cheap stage-2 bank, capital-return surprise re-rates more than EPS — top of range if buyback upsized."*

> **Speed comes from pre-work, not cleverage.** If §7 of the company file (sensitivity), §8 (what's priced/positioning), the life-cycle stage (§3 here), and the relevant tree branch (§5) are all current, the range is a *lookup*, not a calculation.

---

## 7. Calibration — closing the loop

Every range you give, log it (see [`../04_recaps/`](../04_recaps/)). When wrong, classify: *mis-read what was priced / wrong sensitivity / missed positioning / wrong narrative stage / wrong actor-weighting.* Patterns in those errors are where the edge compounds. The scenario tree and the life-cycle map are **living** — update the stage of each name after every print and update branch probabilities as the macro evolves.
